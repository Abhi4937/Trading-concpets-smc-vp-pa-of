Before we examine the elements of the profile more carefully and how to use it in your trading, it's important to understand how professional traders think about markets, price, and volume. For that, we need a brief summary of the auction market theory. Auction market theory states that financial markets work as a continuous auction process through which buyers and sellers negotiate price in order to discover fair value. Buyers and sellers interact in real time according to their opposing self-interest. Buyers want to buy at the lowest possible price, while sellers want to sell at the highest possible price. Because of this, price becomes the point of compromise between these two forces. When buyers and sellers relatively agree on the perception of value, the market tends to move sideways. Price oscillates within a range because both sides consider that range to be fair value. As price rotates back and forth within that range, trading activity accumulates in that area. In the volume profile, this appears as larger horizontal bars because a large amount of trading occurred within that range of prices. This is known as an area of balance or an area of fair value where supply and demand are relatively balanced. But sometimes buyers and sellers strongly disagree about value. When that happens, one side becomes dominant and the market enters what is called price discovery. In this phase, price moves quickly through a wider range as the market searches for a new level where buyers and sellers can agree again. Because price travels quickly through these areas, less trading occur at each individual price level. As a result, these areas appear as smaller bars in the volume profile. Eventually, price finds a new range where trading activity concentrates again and the market returns to balance. Markets move from balance into imbalance and from imbalance back into balance. This process is essentially a price discovery mechanism. The market is always seeking equilibrium, but because new information constantly changes the perception of buyers and sellers, equilibrium is never fully reached. What shifts the balance between supply and demand is the arrival of new information, which alters participants' perception of value relative to price. For example, if new information increases demand, price will enter bullish price discovery. If new information increases supply, price will enter bearish price discovery. In other words, markets are driven by information, not liquidity. Liquidity is simply the mechanism through which changing perceptions of value are translated into price movements. This idea is closely related to what Adam Smith described in The Wealth of Nations as the invisible hand of the market. Individuals pursuing their own self-interest unintentionally create a mechanism that guides markets towards fair value. Buyers try to buy low, sellers try to sell high, and through this interaction, the market discovers the price that reflects perceived value. To summarize, balanced markets are sideways markets where trading activity accumulates. This represents fair value. These areas appear in the volume profile as high volume areas. Imbalanced markets are trending markets where price moves quickly through a range. This represents unfair value or price discovery. These areas appear as low volume areas. The volume profile simply reveals where the market found agreement and where it moved quickly in search of agreement. Given that basic understanding of how the auction market theory relates to the volume profile, let's dive deeper into the elements of the volume profile. The volume profile has two main elements, the value area and the point of control. These ideas are based on the observation that when markets are in balance, the distribution of trading activity across prices often resembles a normal distribution, also known as a bell-shaped curve. In the statistics, the empirical rule states that 68% of observations fall within one standard deviation of the mean. 95% fall within two standard deviations and 99.7% fall within three standard deviations. In the context of the volume profile, traders approximate this principle by defining the value area as the price range that contains 70% of the total trading volume. Meaning the price activity contained in one standard deviation away from the mean. In other words, the value area represents the range of prices where most trading activity occurred. This is the region where buyers and sellers negotiated the most, which is why it's often interpreted as the market's area of fair value. When the market is balanced, price tends to rotate within this region because buyers and sellers largely agree that these prices are reasonable. The point of control, often abbreviated as POC, is the price level where the greatest amount of trading volume occurred. In the profile, it appears as the largest peak in the distribution. Because the POC represents the price where the market spent the most effort trading, it is often considered a very important price level. Here's an important insight that will make all the difference when we look at the volume profile strategies. The 70% value area is considered the broad value area.
