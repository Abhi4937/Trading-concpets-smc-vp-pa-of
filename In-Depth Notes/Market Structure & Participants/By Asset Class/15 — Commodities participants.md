# 15 — Commodities participants

Commodities have a layer no financial market has: the *physical* one. The participants below touch barrels, bushels, and metal — and that is exactly where the durable edge sits. See also [[14 — Derivatives (options & futures) participants]].

## Producers / hedgers (farmers, miners, oil producers)
- **Role:** Owners of future supply who want price certainty.
- **How they trade:** **Sell futures** to lock in a price for output they will deliver later.
- **How they profit:** Not from the future — they accept giving up upside to remove downside; the business is the crop/mine, the future just fixes revenue (📄 sourced).
- **Why they matter:** They are the natural short side of the futures curve.

## Commercial end-users (e.g. airlines hedging jet fuel)
- **Role:** Consumers of the commodity who fear *rising* input costs.
- **How they trade:** **Buy futures** to cap their cost of jet fuel, copper, wheat, etc.
- **Why they matter:** They are the natural long side; producer-sellers and consumer-buyers together form the commercial core of open interest.

## Physical merchant trading houses (Glencore, Vitol, Trafigura, Cargill)
- **Role:** The opaque, hugely profitable middle layer that actually moves the physical commodity.
- **How they profit:** Logistics, storage, blending, and **geographic/temporal arbitrage** — buy where cheap, ship/store/blend, sell where dear; the paper market is used to hedge the physical book (📄 sourced).
- **Why they matter:** They see real-world flows (cargoes, inventories) before the screen does — a genuine information edge.

## CTAs / managed money
- **Role:** Systematic, mostly trend-following speculators.
- **How they profit:** Riding sustained trends; they are momentum, not information.
- **Why they matter:** Their de-leveraging can accelerate reversals; they cluster, creating crowded positioning.

## Commodity index investors
- **Role:** Passive long-only exposure to a basket.
- **How they profit/lose:** Returns include **roll yield** — they bleed in contango, gain in backwardation as they roll expiring contracts.
- **Why they matter:** Predictable monthly roll flows ("the Goldman roll") are front-run by others.

## Warehouses, delivery & assayers
- **Role:** The physical-settlement plumbing — approved storage, delivery logistics, quality certification.
- **Why they matter:** Squeezes and deliveries (e.g. metal warehouse queues) live here; deliverable supply sets the squeeze risk.

## The COT tell
Tie all of the above to the CFTC **commercial vs non-commercial** split. **In commodities the "smart money" are the commercial hedgers who touch the physical** — when they are heavily net-long, supply tightness is often real (📄 sourced). See [[14 — Derivatives (options & futures) participants]].

## What it means for retail
You will never out-info Vitol on a cargo. Trade *with* the commercials' positioning rather than against it, respect roll dates and contango drag, and remember the futures curve encodes storage and delivery realities you can't see on a price chart.

## Limitations
Merchant-house books are private; COT mislabels swap dealers; physical premiums and freight are not on most retail screens, so the curve can move for reasons invisible to you.
