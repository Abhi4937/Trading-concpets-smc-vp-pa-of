# 13 — Stocks & Equities participants

The participants below are *specific* to cash equities and the ETF wrapper. Universal actors (retail, prop firms, generic market makers) are covered elsewhere — see [[06 — Market Makers & Dealers]].

## Designated Market Makers (DMMs) / specialists
- **Role:** Dedicated liquidity providers assigned to specific NYSE-listed securities, with affirmative obligations to maintain a fair and orderly market and to quote at the open/close.
- **How they trade:** They post continuous two-sided quotes and step in during imbalances (notably the opening and closing auctions), using capital to dampen volatility.
- **How they profit:** Bid-ask spread capture plus rebates/parity privileges for their obligation-bearing role.
- **Why they matter:** They are a structural backstop. DMMs accounted for **~17% of liquidity-adding volume in NYSE-listed names in 2019** (✅ verified, NYSE). When you see an orderly open in a thin name, a DMM is usually behind it.

## Passive / index funds & ETF issuers (BlackRock/iShares, Vanguard)
- **Role:** Buy-and-hold owners that replicate an index rather than pick stocks.
- **How they trade:** Mechanical rebalancing to track the benchmark; flows are driven by inflows, not opinion.
- **How they profit:** Expense-ratio fees on assets under management — a scale business, not a trading edge.
- **Why they matter:** They now own a very large share of the market (📄 sourced), so index inclusion/exclusion and rebalance dates create predictable, price-moving flows.

## Authorized Participants (APs)
- **Role:** The only entities permitted to create/redeem ETF shares directly with the issuer.
- **How they trade:** When an ETF trades above NAV they create shares (deliver the basket, receive ETF units) and sell; below NAV they redeem — classic arbitrage.
- **How they profit:** The create/redeem spread versus NAV.
- **Why they matter:** This arbitrage is the mechanism that keeps an ETF price ≈ NAV (✅ verified). It can break under stress, decoupling price from value.

## Activist investors
- **Role:** Funds that take meaningful stakes to force corporate change (board seats, spin-offs, buybacks).
- **How they profit:** Re-rating of the stock after the change is implemented.
- **Why they matter:** A 13D filing can move a stock sharply; they are a catalyst, not a flow.

## Corporate issuers & insiders
- **Role:** The companies themselves, plus executives.
- **How they trade:** **Buybacks** are a huge, persistent source of demand; insiders sell under pre-set **Rule 10b5-1 plans** to avoid the appearance of trading on material non-public information.
- **Why they matter:** Buyback windows and 10b5-1 schedules shape supply/demand independent of fundamentals (📄 sourced).

## IPO underwriters
- **Role:** Investment banks that price, place, and stabilize new issues — see [[07 — Investment Banks (sell-side)]].
- **How they profit:** Underwriting fees plus the greenshoe (over-allotment) option.
- **Why they matter:** They set the float, the price, and the lock-up calendar.

## What it means for retail
You trade against and alongside flows you cannot see: passive rebalances, buyback bids, AP arbitrage. Treat index-rebalance dates, IPO lock-up expiries, and 13D filings as scheduled catalysts. Do not assume the ETF price is "right" in a fast market — the AP arbitrage can lag.

## Limitations
Disclosed ownership and insider filings are lagged; buyback execution is opaque. DMM share figures are venue-specific (NYSE) and do not generalise to Nasdaq's competing-MM model.
