# 06 — Market Makers & Dealers

## What they are
A **market maker** continuously quotes a two-sided market — a **bid** (price to buy) and an **ask** (price to sell) — standing ready to take the other side of trades so that buying and selling can happen at any time. They are the **liquidity providers** of the market 📄. A **dealer** is the closely related role of trading **as principal** (for its own account/inventory), as opposed to a **broker**, which acts only as an **agent** routing your order. See [[08 — Brokers — the business model & conflicts]].

## How they trade / operate
They post quotes, get hit on both sides, and manage the resulting **inventory** to stay roughly neutral. The dominant player illustrates the scale: **Citadel Securities is the largest provider of equities liquidity globally and the largest US options market maker** 📄. They internalize enormous retail flow and quote across thousands of names simultaneously, hedging continuously.

## How they make money (their edge)
Three stacked revenue streams:
1. **The bid–ask spread** — buy at the bid, sell at the ask, repeat across huge volume.
2. **Exchange liquidity rebates** — they are *paid* by venues for posting resting liquidity.
3. **Internalizing uninformed retail flow** — retail orders carry low **adverse-selection risk** (retail rarely knows something the maker doesn't), so taking the other side is close to **riskless** and reliably profitable.

Their edge is **speed, inventory management, and literally being paid to take the other side of retail.** See [[11 — Wholesalers, Internalizers & PFOF]].

## Where they sit in the food chain
Central plumbing. They sit between exchanges and brokers, and between informed and uninformed flow. They are not directional bettors — they want the spread, not the trend — but they are a structural counterparty to nearly every retail click. See [[01 — The Market Food Chain]].

## What it means for a retail trader
When you trade, a market maker is very often the other side, and they have engineered that interaction to be profitable *for them*. The spread you cross and the flow you generate are their revenue. Wider spreads, fast quote fades, and the fact your "instant fill" is someone's edge are all consequences of this.

## Limitations / honest caveats
Market making is genuinely valuable — without it, spreads would be wider and execution slower, so this is **not** pure parasitism. "Near-riskless" describes the aggregate book, not every trade; makers do lose to informed flow and on inventory shocks. The internalization model is also heavily regulated and disclosed.
