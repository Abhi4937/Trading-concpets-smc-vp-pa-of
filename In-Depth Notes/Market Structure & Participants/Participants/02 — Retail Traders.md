# 02 — Retail Traders

## What they are
Retail traders are individuals trading their own personal funds through online brokerage accounts. They have **no direct market access** — they cannot place orders onto an exchange themselves and reach the market only via intermediaries (brokers, who route to wholesalers, exchanges, or internalizers) 📄. They sit at the bottom of the [[01 — The Market Food Chain]]: the smallest capital, the slowest execution, and the least information.

## How they trade / operate
A retail order travels: trader → broker app → broker's routing logic → (often) a wholesaler/market maker or exchange. The trader sees a clean app interface but controls almost nothing about *how* the order is actually executed. Most retail traders are discretionary, undercapitalized, and trade on lagging public information — charts, news, and tips that institutions priced in long ago.

## How they make money (their edge)
Honestly, structurally, they have almost none. Their only durable advantages are **size-independence** (they can enter and exit instantly without moving price), **no mandate** (no benchmark, no redemptions, no career risk), and **patience** (they can sit in cash indefinitely). A disciplined retail trader's edge is behavioral and selective, not informational or technological.

## Where they sit in the food chain
The bottom — and, crucially, the **product being monetized**, not just a participant. Their order flow is sold (PFOF), their spreads are captured, and their fees and financing costs feed every layer above them. See [[22 — How the Industry Profits from Retail Losses]] and [[08 — Brokers — the business model & conflicts]].

## What it means for a retail trader
The documented loss rates are brutal. SEBI found that **93% of Indian individual F&O traders lost money over FY22–FY24**, and only about **1% earned more than ₹1 lakh after costs** 📄. You are not playing against a neutral market; you are the slowest, least-informed participant, and much of the industry's revenue depends on your activity. Treat costs, position size, and selectivity as your only real levers.

## Limitations / honest caveats
The 93% figure is for F&O (derivatives) specifically — long-term equity investing has a different profile. "Retail" also spans a huge range, from a first-week gambler to a well-capitalized, systematic individual. The structural disadvantages are real, but they describe the *average* outcome, not an iron law for every disciplined individual.
