# 04 — Institutions

## What they are
Institutions are the large pools of managed capital: **mutual funds, pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds, and university endowments**. They are long-horizon, mandate-driven, and command enormous AUM (assets under management). They are the dominant "buy-side" of the market — entities that buy securities and execution services rather than sell them 📄.

## How they trade / operate
Because their positions are huge, their core problem is **hiding size**. A pension fund cannot just buy a billion dollars of a stock on the open book without spiking the price. So they trade through **block desks** (negotiated large trades), **execution algorithms** like VWAP and TWAP (slicing an order across the day to track an average price), and **dark pools** (private venues where large orders match without displaying to the public). The goal is to accumulate or distribute quietly over hours or days.

## How they make money (their edge)
Two distinct ways. First — and this is the *real* business — **management fees on AUM**: a fixed percentage of the money they hold, earned whether the fund wins or loses. Second, **long-term returns** for their beneficiaries (pensioners, policyholders, the endowment). Their edge is patient capital, research scale, and mandate stability, not speed.

## Where they sit in the food chain
Near the top by sheer volume. They dominate traded turnover and set much of the supply/demand backdrop. But they are **not your direct adversary** the way a market maker or HFT firm is — they are slow, transparent in aggregate, and trying to *avoid* you, not pick you off. See [[01 — The Market Food Chain]] and [[05 — Hedge Funds, Prop Firms & HFT]].

## What it means for a retail trader
Institutions create the persistent flows behind trends and the liquidity you trade into. Their VWAP/TWAP accumulation can show up as steady, mechanical buying near a level. You will rarely beat them on information, but you can sometimes ride their direction once it is visible.

## Limitations / honest caveats
"Institutions" is a broad bucket — a passive index fund and an actively managed insurer behave nothing alike. The fee-on-AUM model means their incentives are tied to *gathering assets*, not necessarily to outperforming, which is why most active funds underperform their benchmarks net of fees. Dark-pool and block activity is, by design, mostly invisible to you in real time.
