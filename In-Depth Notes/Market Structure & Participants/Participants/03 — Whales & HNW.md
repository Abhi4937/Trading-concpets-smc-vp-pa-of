# 03 — Whales & HNW

## What they are
"Whales" and high-net-worth (HNW) individuals are private participants large enough that their own orders can move price and consume visible liquidity. The term is used everywhere but is most concrete in **crypto**, where individual wallets holding outsized balances are publicly trackable on-chain. In traditional markets the equivalent is the HNW client of a private bank or a large family office.

## How they trade / operate
Unlike a [[02 — Retail Traders|retail trader]], a whale rarely just clicks "buy" on a public order book — that would move the price against them. Instead they get **better broker terms, OTC (over-the-counter) desks, and block execution** that fills large size off-book at a negotiated price. In crypto, large moves still leave on-chain footprints (exchange deposits/withdrawals, wallet transfers) that analytics firms surface in real time.

## How they make money (their edge)
Their edge is **size, patience, access, and sometimes information**. Size lets them negotiate; patience lets them accumulate or distribute over days without panic; access (OTC desks, prime brokerage, deal flow) gives execution and opportunities retail never sees. Occasionally they hold genuine informational advantages — early allocations, private deal terms, or simply being the liquidity everyone else trades against.

## Where they sit in the food chain
Above retail, roughly alongside smaller funds, but below the largest [[04 — Institutions|institutions]] and [[05 — Hedge Funds, Prop Firms & HFT|hedge funds]] in raw AUM. They are price-influencers within specific names or tokens rather than systemic forces. See [[01 — The Market Food Chain]].

## What it means for a retail trader
When a whale moves real size, the **slippage is real** — a large market sell can sweep several price levels, and you may be the resting bid that gets filled. "Whale watching" (following tracked wallets) is a popular crypto strategy, and at its best it flags genuine accumulation or distribution.

## Limitations / honest caveats
Most "whale watching" is **noise**. A large transfer is often an exchange moving its own cold-wallet funds, a custodian rebalancing, or one entity shuffling between its own addresses — not a directional bet. On-chain labels are frequently wrong or stale, and a single whale's trade tells you nothing about *why* they traded. Treat whale data as a weak, contextual signal, never a trigger.
